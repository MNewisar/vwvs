Thahmina Begum

Interdisciplinary artist-researcher · poet · workshop facilitator

Thahmina Begum is a Leeds-based interdisciplinary artist-researcher, poet, and workshop facilitator whose practice explores identity, belonging, migration, memory, and storytelling across British Bangladeshi and Muslim diasporic experiences. Working across printmaking, collage, embroidery, poetry, and participatory art, her work centres community voices, creative wellbeing, and socially engaged practice.

Her work has been exhibited and commissioned by organisations including the British Library, Yorkshire Sculpture Park, Leeds Art Gallery, LEEDS 2023, and the University of Leeds.

LUU and RIBA NorthWest workshops

LUU - Fear and Freedom: Reimagining Safer Spaces for Women in Leeds. Delivered as part of the International Women's Day: Women's Health Symposium, this workshop used emotional mapping and creative prompts to explore safety, confidence, lighting, pavement conditions, public-space comfort and lived experiences in Leeds.

RIBA NorthWest EDI Group - Rethinking Women's Safety: A Gender-Informed Urban Design. This design charrette brought spatial evidence into professional design discussion, focusing on lighting, visibility, mobility, active frontages, spatial comfort and inclusive urban design. Participant-heavy images are anonymised where possible; facilitator documentation is retained.

Be Curious: Reimagining Safe Public Spaces with Children

As part of Smeaton300 at Leeds Industrial Museum, children were invited to reimagine what safe public spaces could look and feel like through drawing, collage, storytelling and spatial imagination.

The workshop encouraged children to reflect on safety, comfort, visibility, play, nature, movement and belonging in the city. Through creative making and conversation, participants explored how public spaces shape emotional experience and everyday life.

The project translated urban research into an accessible participatory activity, foregrounding children's voices in conversations about public space and inclusive design.

Why women’s safety?

Safety is not only crime prevention. It is spatial, emotional and cultural.

These key figures help visitors quickly understand the scale of the issue and why gender-informed urban design matters.

71%of women in the UK have experienced sexual harassment in public areas.
84%of women reported sexual harassment in Leeds city centre in Women Friendly Leeds findings.
95%of women in a Delhi survey reported carrying objects for self-protection.
